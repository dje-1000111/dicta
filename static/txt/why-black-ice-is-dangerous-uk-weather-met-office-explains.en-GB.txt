One of the trickiest winter hazards is something you
often can't see at all.
Roads and pavements could look completely fine,
maybe just a bit wet, but be covered by a thin,
invisible layer of ice.
This is black ice, responsible for
many sudden winter accidents.
So what is black ice?
Black ice is clear ice, sometimes called glaze,
that forms in a very thin layer on dark surfaces
like roads and pavements.
Because it's transparent, you can often see the road
surface through it, which is why people don't realise
it's there until they lose grip.
Black ice usually forms when there's moisture on the ground
and temperatures drop below freezing.
This can happen after rain or melting snow refreezes
overnight. Or when freezing rain or
drizzle freezes on contact with the cold surface.
Even fog or dew can be enough, quietly freezing onto
roads on calm, cold nights.
In some parts of the world, this can happen on a much
larger scale during ice storms.
Prolonged freezing rain can coat entire road networks in
clear ice.
Roads can look wet, but offer almost no
grip. In those conditions, even gentle
slopes become impassable for normal vehicles, and
travel can grind to a halt.
We saw something similar in the UK during the Beast from
the East in 2018, when freezing rain fell
in many places.
In many cases though, that rain landed on top of snow,
creating an icy crust rather than classic black
ice, so while it was extremely slippery, it wasn't always
the visible road-coloured ice we usually mean
by black ice.
Black ice is most common at the end of the night and
early in the morning, especially when temperatures are
close to freezing.
Some places are particularly susceptible, bridges and
overpasses which cool faster, shaded roads which
don't warm up, untreated pavements and rural
lanes. And it often forms in patches, so grip
can disappear suddenly.
For drivers and cyclists, that means you can go from good
grip to almost none in an instant.
One moment your tyres feel fine, the next.
They're gliding over ice.
Even actions like braking suddenly or turning a corner can
be enough to get you sliding.
And if you hit black ice, sudden movements make
things worse.
So slow down gently, steer smoothly
and avoid sharp braking wherever you can.
If temperatures are near zero, it may be safe to assume
black ice could be present even when roads look
completely fine. Slow down without slamming
your brakes on. And take it steady when driving,
cycling, or walking, and keep an eye on the Met
Office forecast as well as any ice warnings.
